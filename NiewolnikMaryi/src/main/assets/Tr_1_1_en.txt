<h3> Treatise on the Perfect Devotion to the Blessed Virgin Mary, Part II, Ch. 3 </h3>
<br/>
To let go of yourself, you have to die on yourself every day, i.e. you have to hold the reins of the soul and the senses of the body, you have to look as if you can't see anything, hear as if you heard nothing, use things
of this world as if they were not used (Cf. 1 Cor 7: 29-31). St. Paul calls it dying everyday: quoditie morior. (See 1 Cor 15:31). If a grain of wheat does not die, it stays on its own. (Jn 12: 24-25). If we do not die ourselves, and if our most sacred religious practices do not lead us to this death, so necessary and at the same time so life-giving, we will not bear fruit useful; our services will be useless, and all our good deeds will be tainted with self-love and willfulness. And that is why the Lord God will abhor our greatest sacrifices and our best deeds, and at the moment of death we will stand with empty hands, that is, without virtues and merits, and there will be no spark of pure love in us. Because such love is possessed only by dead souls, whose lives are hidden with Jesus Christ in God. (Cf. Col 3: 3).
Among all the devotions to the Blessed Mother, one must find the one that will lead us to death soonest, because it will be the best and sanctify us the most. We must remember that not everything that is holy is gold, that not everything that is sweet is honey, that not everything that is easy and what most people do, sanctifies the most. As in the bosom of nature, there are secrets, thanks to which certain natural activities can be performed in a short time with little cost and effort, so there are secrets in the order of grace, thanks to which in a short time one can joyfully and easily fulfill supernatural works, get rid of self-love , be filled with God and become perfect.
The devotion at present is one of these mysteries of grace, unknown to a great number of Christians, known only to a small number of the devout, and practiced and performed by an even smaller number.

<h3> On following Christ, Book III, Ch. 8 </h3>
<br/>
1. Student. Once I conceived, I will speak to my Lord, but I am dust and ashes. (Genesis 18:27) If I would like to consider myself something more, You will stand against me and my transgressions will bear true witness to me, and I cannot deny them. If I acknowledge my littleness and humble myself, letting go of all overconceptions of myself, and consider myself as dust, as it is, Your grace will flow to me and Your light will shine in my heart; then any vanity, even the smallest, will drown in the vastness of my nothingness and perish forever. There you will show me what I am, what I was and where I have gone. And I was turned to ruin, and I did not know. (Ps 72:22). Left to myself, I am impotent and nothingness, but as soon as you look at me, I immediately become strong and a new joy fills me. It is very strange that you so suddenly pick me up and embrace me so kindly, even though my own weight pulls me down. <br> </br>
2. It is caused by your love, which undeserves me undeservedly and supports me in many and many different needs and from countless misfortunes, to be honest, it tears me up outright. I am lost in loving myself badly; Looking only for you and loving you sincerely, I found myself and you at the same time, and through my love for you, I understood even more deeply my own nothingness. For you, most sweetly, deal with me beyond all my merit and above all that I would dare to expect and dare to ask. <br> </br>
3. Be blessed, my God, for although I am not worthy of any of Your graces, Your infinite generosity and goodness never cease to do good, even to the ungrateful and to those who have gone far from You. Convert us to yourselves so that we may be grateful, humble and pious, for You are our salvation, our courage and strength. <br> </br>
