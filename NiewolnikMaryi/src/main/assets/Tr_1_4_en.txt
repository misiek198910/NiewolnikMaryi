<h3> St. Luke 16: 1-13 </h3>
<br/>
THE PARABLE OF THE UNJUSTICY RULER.
<br/>
<br/>
And he said to his disciples, There was a rich man who had a steward; and it was reported to him that he had squandered his goods. And he summoned him and said to him, What is this I hear about thee? Give an account of your lordship, for you will no longer be able to steward. And the steward said to himself, What shall I do, since my lord takes away my stewardship? I cannot dig, I am ashamed to beg. I know what I will do so that when I am composed of the rulership, they will take me to their houses. Therefore, summoning his lord's debtors individually, he said to the first, How much ought you to my lord? And he said, A hundred barrels of oil. And he said to him, Take your record, and sit down at once, and write: fifty. Then he said to another, And you owe how much? And he said, A hundred measures of wheat. And he said to him, Take your record, and write eighty. And the lord praised the unrighteous steward, that he had done prudently; for the children of this world are wiser of their kind than the children of the light. And I say to you, make yourselves friends of the mammon of iniquity, so that when you fail, they may receive you into the eternal places. <br> </br>
Whoever is faithful in the smallest and faithful in the greater; and whoever is unjust in the little is unjust in the greater. So if you have not been faithful in unjust mammon, who will entrust you with true goods? And if you have not been faithful in anyone else, who will give you what is yours? No servant can serve two masters; for either he will hate the one and love the other; or else he will hold to one and despise the other. You cannot serve God and mammon. <br> </br>

<h3> On following Christ, Book I, Ch. 24 </h3>
<br/>
1. In everything, look back at the end, remembering that you will face a strict judge from whom nothing is hidden; He will not be taken away by gifts or persuaded with excuses, but will judge with justice. O most wretched and foolish sinner! What will you answer to God who knows all your wickedness, you who often tremble before an angry man? And why are you not getting ready for the day of judgment, where one will not explain and defend one another, but each will bear his own burden great enough? Now your work is fruitful, your crying is accepted, your groan is heard, and your suffering is satisfaction and purification. <br> </br>
2. The patient man goes through the great and salutary purgatory: when, suffering insults, he grieves more over someone else's anger than over his own harm; when he willingly prays for his adversaries and forgives sins from the heart; when he is reluctant to ask forgiveness from others and is more easily pity than angry; when he often overcomes himself and tries to surrender his body completely to the spirit. It is better to cleanse yourself of sins and faults than to keep them until future purification. Indeed, we deceive ourselves by the disordered love we have for the flesh. <br> </br>
